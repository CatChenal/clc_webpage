E. coli CLC channel: a Cl-/H+ Exchanger

The Chloride Channel Family

The cloning of CLC-0, a voltage-gated Cl- channel of the electrocyte of the Torpedo ray by Jentsch et al. in 1990 led to the identification of the CLC gene family of chloride channels which are physiologically necessary across phylla [1,2].

The CLC family of Cl--transporting proteins encodes both ion channels and Cl-/H+ exchangers [3,4], and mutations in five of the nine human CLC genes lead to genetically inherited diseases [1]. The nine mammalian isoforms are not all voltage-gated and have a split localization: about half in the plasma membranes and the other in the endosomal-lysosomal vesicular membranes [1,6]. In the plasma membrane of cells, CLC channels have a role in determining the resting ΔVm in skeletal muscle fibers, balance salt transport in kidney, inner ear and other epithelial tissues. The exchangers are confined to the membranes of cellular compartments such as endosomes, lysosomes, osteoclastic lacunas, plant vacuoles and synaptic vesicles, or in the bacterial membrane. The CLC transporters play a key role in acidification of the compartments where they reside [6].


(Duran et al., 2010 [6])

One of the three prokaryotic homologues structures available belongs to Escherichia coli's (commonly referred to as CLCec1, or ecCLC here, with pdb id 1OTS & 1KPK). This secondary active antiporter exchanges 2 Cl- for 1 H+[3].

Structurally and biologically, the antiporter is a homodimer whose subunits are made up of 14 helical transmembrane segments with a so-called inverted or anti-parallel topology as characterized by the MacKinnon team (Dutzler et al., 2002 [4,5]), when this group resolved the first crystal structure of this CLC channel homologue.

Variants:

  • P107: found in the chloride channel of plants and is solely responsible for switching the selectivity to nitrate;

  • K147: found in eukaryotic channels

  • GIFAP: found across phyla as GxFxD where x is hydrophopbic

Hypothesis:

If there are proteins, not identified as chloride channels even in a putative way, that contain this highly conserved Cl- (or anion) selective motif, they are also channels.


Methods:

  1. Search motif selection:

    2. The essential residues described above can be searched for in annotated databases using the following regular expressions (among many other combinations):

    1. G-[SP]-G-I-P
    2. G-[SP]-G-I-P-x(19)-K: includes the intervening lysine
    3. G-[RK]-E-G-P
    4. K-x(14)-G-[RK]-E-G-P: includes the intervening lysine
    5. G-y-F-y-P: y = hydrophobic residue
    6. G-y-F-y-P-x(89)-Y445: includes trailing tyrosine

    However, half of these combinations, b, d and f, would somewhat biais the results towards homolgues and/or are either too common a motif (e) or are beyond the computing capabilities of the search algorithms (f). Because of this, I have selected to perform two motif searches using a and c.


    • Pattern search engine used:
      • http://myhits.isb-sib.ch/cgi-bin/pattern_search#

    • Database of proteins:
      • Complete proteomes from Archaea [arch]
      • Complete proteomes from Bacteria [bact]
      • Complete proteomes from Eukayota [euk]

  2. Analysis:

    3. The search results consist of two sets (lists) of proteins: the one containing motif a, the other containing motif c, therefore I eliminated from both list those proteins described with the following key words:

    • chloride channel or chloride-channel
    • chloride ion channel
    • chloride transporter
    • cl-channel
    • clc family
    • chloride transport

    The filtering was done using UNIX utilities & the final files comparison was done via an awk script, which was first tested on two files of known composition:

    awk -f ~/bin/getcommon.awk GSGIP-final.hits GREGP-final.hits

    The proteins present in both reduced sets (G[RK]EGP-final and G[SP]GIP-final), should therefore be either unknown proteins, or proteins identified with different keywords hence, presumably, with different functions.

Conclusion:

The doublet of 5-residue-long motifs that was used to search the complete proteomes of Archea, Bacteria and Eukaryota is sufficient to recover the entire CLC channel class of proteins, including those putatively assigned to it. This is rather remarkable since, within the CLC class, the proteins are made up of over 450 residues if they are Prokaryotes, or over 600 residues if they are Eukaryotes.


Bioinformatic Project - Part II

Modeling missing residues in the Eukaryote structure

To date, there is only one structure of a eukaryotic chloride/proton exchanger. This chloride channel protein called cmCLC belongs to a unicellular, thermophilic red alga, Cyanidioschyzon merolae, and its structure was determined to a 3.5 Å resolution in 2010 by the MacKinnon group [8; 3ORG.pdb].

Unfortunately, the first 85 residues were lost through hydrolysis during the purification of this eukaryotic homologue of the E.coli chloride/proton exchanger (ecCLC). EcCLC has been crystallized to a 2.5 Å resolution in 2003 also by the same group [4].


Structural particularities of 3ORG:

The authors have expressed "[o]n the basis of limited proteolysis, a final construct excluding 86 N-terminal and 7 C-terminal amino acids", as stated in their report[8]. However, this statement is not quite straightforward since one has to consult the Supplementary Information to find out that the 7 terminal residues are not merely missing, but that they have been replaced by 9 residues belonging to a protease pre-scission recognition sequence used in the cleavage of an attached GFP at the C-terminal.

Using the SEQ record of the pdb, along with the COBALT alignment of 3orga and CDS3234 (its parent sequence), I have been able to pinpoint these discrepancies:

  • N-terminal:
    • The first 86 residues were hydrolized, then a Met residue subsequently capped the N-terminal (so that M1 in ORG is R86 in CDS3264, the parent sequence); this is not stated outright in the paper, only in the Supplemental Information.

  • C-terminal:
    • Below is the sequence alignment of the tailing residues of the C-terminal of 3orga and its parent showing the 9-residue mutation:

      3ORG-a   YGYSNSLEVLFQ  632
            SNSLEVLFQ : protease PreScission recognition sequence
CDS3264  YGYREPEQTS--  715
            123456789

In the context of this short bioinformatic project, I thought these terminal losses would provide for a succinct evaluation of several protein structure modeling programs, and this for two reasons:

  • Two structures from Prokaryotes (Salmonella typhimurium [1KPL] and Escherichia coli [1OTS]) are expected to be found as templates by all algorithms.

  • These templates are only partial templates since a number of N-terminal residues (1-16) are 'missing' due to non-resolution of the x-ray diffraction patterns in that region (but the proteins have been expressed and purified in full form).

Hypotheses:

  1. Considering the above in addition to the fact that the two N-terminal segments have only 40% identity, to what extent in coverage and RMSD can the models recover the missing residues? Because the core, or transmembrane domain of the protein is all alpha-helical, the hypothesis is that all models should recover the structure.

  2. 3ORG, as characterized by the MacKinnon group, is a functional exchanger and its dimer state is presumed from the crystal structure. Yet, because of the extent of its N-ter deletion and C-ter modification, it is far from wild-type. A second hypothesis of this modeling project is the following: the finding of a reliable, full length model will be the second weakness of the proposed mechanistic explanation for the chloride/proton exchange in 3ORG hence, via homolgy, in all eukaryotic CLCs. Its first weakness is to be based on a "functional" protein whose length is only 88% of its wild-type, and the second will relate to the possiblity that the missing N-terminal portion is found to interact with it's C-terminal. This is a plausible situation since several of ecCLC's N-ter residues are H-bonding with C-ter residues of the other monomer as it is visible in the overlay picture of 1OTS and 3ORG (Figure 1.)

  1. Model submission:

    2. Because of the structural peculiarities considered above, I submitted 3ORG, along with CDS3264 (i.e. wt-3ORG), for modeling.
